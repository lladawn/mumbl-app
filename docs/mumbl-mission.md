# why mumbl exists

---

## the observation

washing dishes alone is a chore.

washing dishes with someone you love, talking about nothing important,
is somehow one of the best parts of the day.

the task didn't change. the human context around it did.

work is the same. the code review is the same code review. the standup
is the same standup. the ticket is the same ticket. but if you're doing
it with people you actually like — people who get your jokes, who you
want to see succeed, who you'd grab a coffee with if you lived in the
same city — the whole texture of the day changes. you're not counting
down to 6pm anymore. you're just living.

that's not a soft observation. that's the difference between someone
doing the job and someone doing their best work.

---

## the problem

gallup has tracked employee engagement for decades across millions of
workers worldwide. the number has barely moved. roughly 23% of people
are engaged at work. the other 77% are either checked out or actively
miserable.

not because they're lazy. not because the work isn't meaningful.
because work, for most people, doesn't feel human enough to care about
fully.

we call it "work-life balance" — as if work and life are opponents
you have to keep separated. the goal becomes escape: get through the
day so you can get to the real stuff. the family. the friends. the
things that actually feel like living.

but most people spend 8 to 10 hours a day at work. that *is* life.
the biggest part of it. and most people are spending that part either
performing, surviving, or waiting for it to end.

that's not a small problem. that's the default human experience of
employment. and almost nobody is trying to fix it at the root.

---

## why it's hard

the reason work culture is bad in most places isn't that companies
are evil or that people are weak. it's simpler than that.

**human connection at work is treated as a byproduct.**

something that either happens naturally or doesn't. something that
HR runs a team event for once a quarter. something that's assumed to
be fine until someone quits and the exit interview reveals it wasn't.

early stage startups have good culture — not because they're special,
but because when there are 8 people figuring out how to survive
together, human connection happens by accident. you can't avoid knowing
your coworkers when you're all in the same room. the culture is an
accidental output of proximity and shared struggle.

scale breaks that. at 50 people it starts fraying. at 200 it's mostly
gone. at 2000 most people don't know who works three teams over. and
remote work removed even the accidental proximity that used to hold
small teams together.

so the problem isn't intent. most companies genuinely want good
culture. they just have no infrastructure for human connection at
scale.

they have slack for work.
they have email for decisions.
they have nothing for the actual human stuff.

---

## the insight

when people actually connect with who they work with —

not professionally connect. not network. not "build relationships
as a strategic career move."

just *like* the people they work with. know them a little. find them
funny. feel found by them in return —

the work gets better. the output gets better. the problems get solved
faster because people trust each other enough to say what they actually
think. the quiet engineer speaks up because they feel safe enough to.
the hard week is more bearable because someone in the feed noticed.

this isn't idealism. this is how humans work. we do our best alongside
people we feel connected to. always have.

the chores analogy holds at every scale. the most boring task becomes
bearable — sometimes even good — when you're doing it with people you
actually want to be around. the code isn't less complex. the deadline
isn't less real. but the context around it is human, and that changes
everything.

---

## what we're building

mumbl is the infrastructure for human connection at work that doesn't
exist yet.

not another productivity tool. not another HR platform. not a survey
that goes to management. not a forced fun initiative.

a space. for the actual human stuff. the thought you had at 2pm that
didn't fit in a ticket. the thing that was funny in a PR review. the
hard week you're having that you didn't want to announce in standup.
the find that made your day interesting. the win that nobody else saw.

a space where the quiet engineer finally says something and the team
is glad they did. where the person who works three timezones away
starts to feel like a real person and not just a username in a
standup doc. where the team that's never met in person starts to
actually know each other.

and over time — as teams use it, as heartbeats accumulate, as
patterns emerge across hundreds of spaces — a way to understand
what actually makes work bad and fix it before the damage is done.

not as surveillance. as insight. as the thing that finally gives
the human experience of work somewhere to go and something to do
about it.

---

## the mission

> to make work feel like something worth being in.
> not by making it less work —
> but by making it more human.

to scale the feeling that early stage startups have by accident —
where everyone knows each other, where the work feels alive, where
you actually want to show up — to every team, everywhere, regardless
of size or culture or whether anyone in leadership has ever thought
about this.

to give people back the biggest part of their day.

not as an escape. as a life.

---

## the way we do it

one team at a time.

the mission is big. the method is small. a space for one team of
engineers to have a real conversation. so good that they tell another
team. and that team tells another.

we don't fix work culture by announcing we're fixing work culture.
we fix it by making one team's tuesday feel a little more human than
it did last week. and then another team's. and then another.

the scale follows the product working — not the product trying to be
the mission all at once.

---

## what success looks like

not a metric. a moment.

an engineer who has never said anything in a standup drops something
honest in the feed. three people react. they feel — for the first time
in a long time at work — like they're actually there with people who
notice.

that's it. that's the whole thing.

get that right for enough teams and the world's work culture starts
to look different.
